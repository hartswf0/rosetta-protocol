The Arena of Struggle

A Stuart Hall-Inspired Analysis of the Jimmy Kimmel Affair

**The Conjuncture:** ABC’s suspension and reinstatement of Jimmy Kimmel amid pressure from Trump-aligned actors triggered a cross-platform fight over free-speech norms and institutional authority.

1. Periodisation: Identifying the Breaks

Stuart Hall on... Periodisation

“Difficult problems are posed here by périodisation... Are the major breaks largely descriptive? Do they arise largely from within popular culture itself, or from factors which are outside of but impinge on it? ... I select this period – the 1880s-1920s – because it is one of the real test cases... a period of deep structural change.”

Hall insists that we must identify the specific historical moments, or "breaks," where the rules of the game change. We must ask what caused these shifts—were they internal to culture, or caused by external pressures like politics, technology, or economics?

Show Definition...

Applying this, the Kimmel affair isn't one long event, but a series of ruptures. We can identify two key breakpoints where the terrain of struggle was reconstituted:

  • Breakpoint 1 (Sep 15–17): The Act of Containment. The initial remarks are met with external pressure (FCC, Trump) leading to a corporate action (ABC's suspension). This is a moment of disciplinary "reform" and "proscription," where the power-bloc actively pushes Kimmel's expression to the margins.
  • Breakpoint 2 (Sep 23–24): The Act of Resistance & Re-appropriation. Kimmel’s return is not a simple continuation. The monologue, combined with the massive digital audience bypassing affiliate blackouts, constitutes a qualitative change. The "apparatus" of distribution shifts from broadcast TV to social platforms, re-centering the discourse on Kimmel's terms.

2. Defining The 'Popular'

Stuart Hall on... The 'Popular'

“I settle for a third definition of ‘popular’... what is essential to the definition of popular culture is the relations which define ‘popular culture’ in a continuing tension (relationship, influence and antagonism) to the dominant culture... It has at its centre the changing and uneven relations of force... the question of cultural struggle and its many forms.”

For Hall, "popular culture" is not a fixed set of items or authentic folk traditions. It is a battlefield, defined by the constant tension and struggle between the culture of the dominant power-bloc and the culture of "the people" (an alliance of subordinated classes and groups).

Show Definition...

The Kimmel affair is a perfect example of this definition in action. It is not simply about whether late-night comedy is "good" or "bad." It is about the struggle over its meaning and function:

  • The Power-Bloc's Definition: Attempts to define Kimmel's monologue as illegitimate, dangerous speech that falls outside the bounds of acceptable broadcast content. Their goal is to contain it.
  • The Popular Forces' Definition: An alliance of entertainers, audiences, and even some free-speech conservatives worked to define the monologue as a legitimate, even necessary, form of satire—a core part of a democratic "popular" tradition. Their goal was to resist containment.

The very meaning of Kimmel's show became the prize to be won in this cultural struggle.

3. The Dialectic of Cultural Struggle

Stuart Hall on... The Dialectic

“In the study of popular culture, we should always start here: with the double-stake in popular culture, the double movement of containment and resistance, which is always inevitably inside it... Cultural struggle, of course, takes many forms: incorporation, distortion, resistance, negotiation, recuperation.”

Hall argues that culture is a constant "double movement." The dominant culture tries to contain, manage, and "reform" popular forms (Containment). At the same time, popular forces push back, challenge, and resist these efforts (Resistance). This is a perpetual, dynamic struggle, not a one-time event.

Show Definition...

We can model the media artifacts in the Kimmel affair along this dialectic. Every article, statement, and video clip can be seen as a move in this game:

  • Mechanisms of Containment: The power-bloc used tactics of **Ban/Proscription** (affiliate blackouts), **Policing** (FCC threats), and attempts at **Incorporation** (framing Kimmel as an out-of-touch elite, thus neutralizing his "popular" appeal).
  • Mechanisms of Resistance: The popular forces used the **Commercial Insertion** of viral clips to bypass the ban, **Appropriation** by reframing the debate around the First Amendment, and building a cross-ideological coalition to resist incorporation.

4. Articulation & The Arena of the Sign

Stuart Hall on... Articulation & The Sign

“What matters is not the intrinsic or historically fixed objects of culture, but the state of play in cultural relations... The meaning of a cultural symbol is given in part by the social field into which it is incorporated, the practices with which it articulates and is made to resonate... Sign becomes an arena of class struggle.” (Citing Volosinov)

Hall argues that cultural symbols have no fixed meaning. Their meaning is created by "articulating" them—linking them—to certain ideas, feelings, or political projects. The cultural struggle is a fight over these articulations. A swastika can be articulated to fascism or, bizarrely, to punk rebellion. The sign itself is an "arena of struggle."

Show Definition...

In this conflict, key signs and terms were battlegrounds:

  • "Free Speech": The power-bloc tried to articulate this to mean "freedom from consequences for harmful speech." The popular forces articulated it as "freedom from government censorship and corporate cowardice." The struggle was over which definition would become common sense.
  • "Jimmy Kimmel": The man himself became a sign. Was he an "elite liberal mouthpiece" (the power-bloc's articulation) or a "brave satirist defending democracy" (the popular forces' articulation)?
  • "The People": As Hall notes, both sides claim to speak for "the people." The Trump administration claimed "the people" were offended. Kimmel's supporters claimed "the people" value free expression and tuned in by the millions to prove it. The struggle was to constitute and claim this powerful political subject.
